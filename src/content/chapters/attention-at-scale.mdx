Chapter 13's generation loop was honest about its cost: every token
sampled means one full forward pass, restarted from scratch, through the
entire stack. Its footer flagged the obvious waste inside that restart:
recomputing every earlier token's key and value vectors, over and over,
when nothing about them had changed, and promised the fix would get its
own chapter. This is that chapter. The fix is called the **KV cache**,
and it's less a clever trick than an accounting decision. Since a
token's key and value are pure functions of that token and everything
before it, compute them once, store them, and never touch that
computation again.

Step through the figure above and the shape of the win is concrete. With
no cache, generating a sequence's sixth token means recomputing all six
tokens' keys and values, and the fifth token's step recomputed five, and
so on: the running total is 1+2+3+4+5+6, a triangular number, growing
quadratically with sequence length. With a cache, every single step does
exactly one unit of fresh work and reuses the rest. O(T²) becomes O(T).
That's not a marginal optimization; for long sequences it's the
difference between a model that serves acceptably and one that doesn't
serve at all. But read the figure's third step as the honest asterisk:
the compute you saved didn't vanish, it *moved*, into memory. A cache
that never shrinks and only grows is now resident on the GPU for as long
as the conversation runs.

That memory bill is exactly what the calculator half of the figure
prices out, and the three presets are three real answers to the same
question: how much can you shrink it without giving up quality?
**MHA** (multi-head attention) is the no-sharing baseline: every query
head keeps its own dedicated key and value head, so the cache scales
with the *full* head count, and at real context lengths the number
explodes past anything reasonable. **GQA** (grouped-query attention),
the choice behind the Llama-70B family, has many query heads share one
key/value head. Llama-70B-scale models publish 8 KV heads, a flat 16×
reduction against MHA's 128 for a small, well-measured quality cost.
**MLA** (multi-head latent attention) goes further and changes the
approach entirely: instead of shrinking the *number* of key/value heads,
DeepSeek's published design compresses each token's key and value into
one shared low-rank latent vector per layer, small enough to store
cheaply, and reconstructs the full-size keys and values from it on
demand at attention time. Published DeepSeek/K2-family figures put the
result at roughly another 12× smaller than GQA's already-reduced
footprint: real numbers from a real published architecture, not a
theoretical ceiling.

Drag the context slider toward 128k and beyond and watch what "explodes"
actually means: MHA's bar runs off scale while GQA and MLA stay
survivable, because both attack the same term in the formula (the
per-token, per-layer footprint) from different angles, sharing versus
compressing. Worth noticing what neither one touches: attention's *compute*
cost is still the quadratic $T^2$ chapter 10 introduced, comparing every
query against every key. The KV cache is a memory fix layered on top of
that unchanged compute cost, not a replacement for it. They are two
separate bills, attacked by mostly separate engineering.

Long context has a second ceiling worth naming honestly, back at
position encoding rather than memory. Chapter 12's rotary position
embeddings were fit to whatever range of positions the model actually
trained on; push past that range and the rotations start extrapolating
into territory the model never learned to read. Labs stretch that
ceiling after training, adjusting RoPE's base frequency, or fine-tuning
briefly at the new target length, rather than retraining a frontier
model from scratch just to widen its window. And sparse or windowed
attention variants, which skip most of that $T^2$ comparison instead of
computing it in full, absolutely exist and get published regularly. But
as of this writing, dense, full attention still wins at the frontier
more often than not. Simplicity and a well-understood cost model have
kept beating cleverer approximations, so far.

<MathAside>

KV cache size in bytes: $\text{bytes} = 2 \cdot L \cdot H_{kv} \cdot
d_{head} \cdot p \cdot T$, where $L$ is the number of layers, $H_{kv}$
the number of key/value heads, $d_{head}$ the width of one head, $p$ the
bytes per stored number (2 for fp16), and $T$ the number of cached
tokens. The leading 2 is keys *and* values, both stored, both counted.

GQA changes exactly one factor: $H_{kv}$ shrinks from the full head count
down to a small shared group count, everything else in the formula
untouched. MLA changes a different factor: it replaces the whole product
$H_{kv} \cdot d_{head}$ with a single compressed latent dimension, small
by design, reconstructed back up to full size only at the moment
attention actually needs it. The compression lives in what gets stored,
not in what gets computed against.

</MathAside>

<EngineerFooter>

Serving a large language model is, underneath the model-shaped surface,
a memory-bandwidth business. Every generated token needs its cache read
off the GPU before the next matrix multiply can even start, and that
read scales with three numbers multiplied together: batch size, context
length, and per-token KV bytes. Batch size is easy to forget here: it's
not a slider in this chapter's figure, but every concurrent conversation
a server is handling multiplies the same cache cost again, which is
exactly why production inference stacks obsess over KV-cache-efficient
attention variants. It's the line item that decides how many users one
GPU can actually hold in memory at once.

That's also the honest answer to why "1 million token context window"
reads as a headline. It is not evidence of a smarter model: the
attention mechanism inside a 1M-context model is the identical
$\text{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k})V$ from chapter 10. It's evidence of
a systems team that solved a memory-bandwidth and engineering problem,
compressing caches and building smarter serving with enough GPU memory
bandwidth to keep that much state alive per user. Read a context-length
headline as a statement about infrastructure investment, not
architectural insight. This chapter's calculator is exactly the
arithmetic that headline is quietly asking you to trust.

</EngineerFooter>
