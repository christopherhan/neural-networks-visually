Chapter 8 ended on a promise: instead of carrying a compressed memory
forward step by step, a network could simply *look back* at any part of a
sequence, directly, whenever it needs to. Part III is that promise kept.
But "look back at the sequence" quietly assumes something hasn't been
settled yet. Look back at *what*? A neuron computes weighted sums and
matrix multiplies; it has no idea what a letter is. Before attention can
reach across a sentence, the sentence has to become numbers it can reach
across. That's this chapter's whole job, and it turns out to be less
obvious than it sounds.

There are two naive answers, and both of them break. **Feed it individual characters.**
The vocabulary stays tiny (a few dozen symbols) and nothing is ever
out-of-vocabulary, but a paragraph explodes into hundreds of tokens, and
every one of them is nearly meaningless on its own: the network has to
spend enormous effort just re-discovering that "t-h-e" is a word before it
can think about what the word *means*. **Feed it whole words instead.**
Now each token actually means something, but the vocabulary balloons
toward unbounded: every name, typo, and made-up word needs its own slot,
and words that obviously share structure ("run", "running", "runner") get
completely unrelated ids, so nothing learned about one transfers to the
others. Too fine-grained and everything is noise; too coarse and the
vocabulary can't hold still.

**Byte-pair encoding (BPE)** splits the difference, and it does it by
*learning* the vocabulary from data instead of guessing it in advance.
Start with individual characters as the only tokens. Scan a large body of
text and find the pair of adjacent symbols that occurs together most
often, say `t` and `h`. Merge every occurrence of that pair into a single
new symbol, `th`. Now repeat: find the next most frequent adjacent pair
(maybe `th` and `e`, giving `the`) and merge it too. Do this a few hundred
times and something useful falls out automatically: strings that show up
constantly, like "the" and "ing", earn their own single token, cheaply,
while anything rare stays broken into smaller pieces. Nothing is ever
truly out-of-vocabulary, because in the worst case a word just falls all
the way back to individual characters. It stays *spellable*, even if it
isn't efficient.

Type a sentence and watch where the seams fall. Common words like "the" or
"and" usually ride as a single whole chip. Try something rarer:
"extraordinarily" next to "the" is a good pair to compare. Watch it
shatter into three or four pieces, each one a chunk this exact vocabulary
happened to learn was worth keeping.

Tokenizing gets text down to a sequence of integers, but an integer id
carries no relationships: id 47 isn't "closer" to id 48 than it is to id
9000. The next step is to give every id a **learned vector**: a row in a
big trainable matrix, looked up by id, called an **embedding**. Nothing
about that vector is hand-designed. It starts random, and the only
pressure on it is the network's single training objective, predicting the
next token, applied over and over across a whole corpus. That turns out to
be enough: if two tokens tend to show up in similar contexts, the gradient
nudges their vectors in similar directions each time, and over millions of
updates they drift toward each other in the embedding space. "Meaning," in
this scheme, is nothing more than geometry that training discovered on its
own.

<EmbeddingMap client:visible />

This is the real embedding space this site's tiny model learned, flattened
from 64 dimensions down to 2 so it fits on a page. Some structure survives
the squashing, some doesn't. Search for a few tokens and see what lands
near what: punctuation marks tend to huddle together, pronouns cluster
with other pronouns, and dialogue verbs like "said" and "cried," the words
that report *how* something was spoken in these storybooks, tend to keep
close company too. Nobody told the model that punctuation is a category or
that "cried" is a cousin of "said." It fell out purely from which tokens
kept appearing in similar neighborhoods.

Worth sizing this honestly. This site's tokenizer has a vocabulary of 383
tokens and 64-dimensional embeddings, trained on three short storybooks.
Production language models use the same exact recipe at a very different
scale: vocabularies around 100,000 tokens, embeddings thousands of
dimensions wide, trained on a meaningful fraction of the internet. Nothing
about the *mechanism* changes; only the size of the matrices and the size
of the data pushing on them.

<MathAside>

An embedding lookup is a matrix multiply in disguise: represent token id
$i$ as a one-hot vector $e_i \in \mathbb{R}^V$ (all zeros except a 1 at
position $i$), and $e_i^\top E$ against the embedding matrix $E \in
\mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ picks out exactly row $i$: the lookup *is* the
matrix product, just computed by indexing instead of by actually
multiplying zeros. Vocabulary size $V$ and sequence length $T$ trade
against each other: a coarser tokenizer (small $V$) needs more tokens to
say the same thing (larger $T$ for a given passage), while a finer one
(large $V$) shortens $T$ at the cost of a bigger embedding table. This
site's $V = 383$ is trained on a corpus of three Gutenberg books, about
547KB of raw text, tiny by production standards, but the merge algorithm
and the lookup are identical to what ships in a frontier model.

</MathAside>

<EngineerFooter>

Tokenizer quirks are the classic LLM footgun, and now you can see exactly
why. Ask a model to count the r's in "strawberry" and it may get it
wrong, not because it can't count, but because it never *sees*
"strawberry" as eight characters; it sees one or two token ids, and the
letters inside a token are invisible to everything downstream. The same
blind spot shows up in arithmetic quirks, where "380" and "38" and "0"
might tokenize in inconsistent, non-place-value-aligned ways depending on
context. The embedding table itself is unglamorous: it's just a $(V
\times d)$ matrix of trainable numbers, functioning as the model's literal
first layer, nothing more exotic than that. And the BPE
merge-the-most-frequent-pair algorithm in this chapter's demo isn't a
simplification for teaching purposes; it's exactly what OpenAI's
`tiktoken` and Google's `sentencepiece` run in production, just
industrialized: bigger corpora, bigger vocabularies, heavily optimized
merge implementations, same core idea.

</EngineerFooter>
